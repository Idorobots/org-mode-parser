** Digit counter — period terminator

Method for a classic beurre blanc:

1. Combine 120 ml dry white wine and 60 ml white wine vinegar in a small saucepan.
2. Add two finely minced shallots and a pinch of white pepper.
3. Reduce over medium heat until only 2 tablespoons of liquid remain.
4. Remove from heat and let cool to approximately 60°C.
5. Add 250 g cold unsalted butter, one cube at a time, whisking constantly.
6. Return briefly to very low heat if the emulsion cools too much.
7. Strain through a fine sieve and season with salt and lemon juice.
8. Keep warm in a bain-marie at 55°C until service.

** Digit counter — parenthesis terminator

Steps to sharpen a knife on a whetstone:

1) Soak the 1000-grit stone in water for ten minutes.
2) Hold the blade at a consistent 15–20° angle to the stone.
3) Push the edge forward in smooth arcs, heel to tip, ten strokes per side.
4) Move to the 3000-grit stone and repeat with lighter pressure.
5) Finish on the 6000-grit stone with minimal pressure, eight strokes per side.
6) Strop on leather five times per side.
7) Test sharpness by slicing a sheet of paper cleanly.

** Letter counter — period terminator

Wine pairing principles (from lightest to most complex):

a. Sparkling wines before still; dry before off-dry.
b. White wines before red; light reds before heavy reds.
c. Match weight: delicate dishes with delicate wines.
d. Bridge flavours: acidic wines with acidic dishes.
e. Contrast when bridge fails: salty cheese with sweet Sauternes.

* Descriptive Lists

Regional flour varieties and their uses:

- "00" flour :: Ultra-fine Italian wheat flour; ideal for fresh pasta and Neapolitan pizza dough. Protein content 9–12%.
- Bread flour :: High-gluten wheat flour (12–14% protein); gives chew and structure to yeast-leavened breads.
- Cake flour :: Very low protein (7–9%); produces tender crumb in layer cakes and delicate pastries.
- Semolina :: Coarse-ground durum wheat; used for dried pasta, gnocchi alla romana, and polenta-style porridge.
- Buckwheat :: Gluten-free pseudo-grain flour; earthy and slightly bitter; used in blinis, soba noodles, and galettes.
- Chestnut :: Naturally sweet, gluten-free; pairs well with honey and aged cheeses; used in Corsican polenta and castagnaccio.
- Teff :: Tiny Ethiopian grain, iron-rich and slightly molasses-flavoured; the base of injera flatbread.

Equipment terms:

- Bain-marie :: A water bath used to apply gentle, indirect heat — essential for custards, melting chocolate, and holding sauces.
- Chinois :: A very fine conical strainer for clarifying stocks and sauces to a mirror finish.
- Mandoline :: An adjustable slicing tool for producing uniform, paper-thin cuts of vegetables and fruit.
- Tamis :: A drum sieve used to aerate flour or pass cooked vegetables into a smooth purée.

** Nested ordered lists

Full recipe structure for Bouillabaisse:

1. Prepare the stock
   1. Sweat onion, fennel, and leek in olive oil until soft (8 minutes).
   2. Add garlic, tomato paste, and saffron; cook 2 minutes.
   3. Add fish bones and heads; cover with cold water.
   4. Simmer 25 minutes — do not boil or the stock will turn cloudy.
   5. Strain, discard solids, reserve stock.
2. Prepare the rouille
   a. Pound garlic and salt to a paste in a mortar.
   b. Add saffron, soaked in a teaspoon of warm water.
   c. Work in egg yolk, then drizzle in olive oil to emulsify.
   d. Season with lemon juice, cayenne, and pepper.
3. Cook the seafood
   1. Bring stock to a rolling boil.
   2. Add firm fish (monkfish, John Dory) and cook 4 minutes.
   3. Add delicate fish (red mullet, sea bream) and cook 2 minutes.
   4. Add mussels and clams; cook until they open (2–3 minutes).
4. Serve
   a. Place toasted baguette slices in each bowl.
   b. Spread rouille generously on toast.
   c. Ladle hot broth and fish over the toast.
   d. Serve remaining rouille on the side.
