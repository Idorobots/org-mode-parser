#+TITLE: A Brief History of Punctuation — Footnote Showcase
#+AUTHOR: Quorra Briskett

* Standard Footnote References and Definitions

The comma, whose origins can be traced to the Greek scholars of Alexandria,[fn:1]
was not used consistently in European manuscripts until the advent of movable
type printing in the fifteenth century.[fn:2]

The semicolon is a relative latecomer among punctuation marks.[fn:semicolon]
It first appeared in Italian humanist printing around 1494[fn:3] and was
systematised by Aldus Manutius the Elder,[fn:aldus] whose press in Venice
became the model for scholarly typography across Europe.

The em dash — beloved by writers, despised by copy editors[fn:4] — has no
single origin story. It derives from a longer tradition of manuscript
annotation marks.[fn:5]

[fn:1] Aristophanes of Byzantium (c. 257–180 BCE) is often credited with
devising the earliest systematic punctuation system for Greek texts, using
three levels of dot placement to indicate short, medium, and long pauses.

[fn:2] The earliest printed books, incunabula, often had no punctuation at all,
leaving spaces for readers or scribes to add marks by hand. Gutenberg's
42-line Bible (c. 1455) is a notable early example.

[fn:semicolon] The word "semicolon" itself is a compound of the Greek /semi/ (half)
and /colon/ (member or clause). Its modern name did not stabilise until the
seventeenth century in English usage.

[fn:3] Specifically, Pietro Bembo's /De Aetna/ (1495), printed by Aldus Manutius,
is frequently cited as the earliest surviving printed work to use the semicolon
consistently. The date is sometimes given as 1494 in sources that use the old
calendar.

[fn:aldus] Aldus Manutius (1449–1515) also invented the italic typeface and the
portable octavo book format, transforming books from lectern objects to pocket
companions. See [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldus_Manutius][his Wikipedia article]] for an overview.

[fn:4] The Chicago Manual of Style (17th ed., 2017) devotes §6.85–6.89 to the em
dash, noting that it "is perhaps the most versatile punctuation mark" while
cautioning against overuse.

[fn:5] Specifically, the medieval /positura/ system included marks resembling
modern dashes used to indicate pauses at the end of a clause.

* Inline Footnotes

Inline footnotes embed the definition directly at the point of reference.

The interrobang[fn:interrobang:The interrobang (‽) is a non-standard punctuation
mark combining the functions of a question mark and an exclamation point. It was
proposed by Wulbert Grumshaw in 1962 and has never achieved wide adoption in
formal writing.] is the most whimsical of all punctuation marks.

The Oxford comma[fn:oxford:Also called the serial comma or Harvard comma, it is
the comma placed before the conjunction in a list of three or more items. Its
use is *mandatory* in American English by most style guides, but /discouraged/
in British English by many publishers.] divides writers into two irreconcilable camps.

The pilcrow (¶)[fn:pilcrow:The pilcrow derives from the Latin /capitulum/ (chapter)
via Old French /paragraphe/. In medieval manuscripts, it was drawn in red or blue
ink by a rubricator — a specialist scribe — to mark paragraph breaks. See also:
[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilcrow][Wikipedia on the pilcrow]].] marks
the paragraph in proofreading and older typography conventions.

An inline footnote with a very short definition: the slash is called a
solidus[fn:solidus:From Latin /solidus/, a gold coin whose abbreviation /s/ was
written in the numerator position of the price fraction.] in formal contexts.

* Anonymous Footnotes

Anonymous footnotes have no label and cannot be referenced by name — each
occurrence is a fresh footnote.

The question mark originated in medieval scribal practice[fn::The Latin word
/quaestio/ (question) was abbreviated as =qo=, which scribes eventually
combined into a single glyph — a =q= over an =o= — that gradually evolved
into the modern =?=.] and is now universal across writing systems.

The exclamation mark has a similarly murky history.[fn::One theory holds that
it derives from the Latin word /io/ (an exclamation of joy), written vertically
with the =i= above the =o= — again a scribal compression that became a glyph.
Another theory links it to medieval manuscript =nota= (note) marks.]

Both anonymous footnotes above are distinct even though they appear in the same
section. They will receive sequential numbers in the rendered output.

* Footnotes with Complex Content

Standard footnotes can contain multiple paragraphs, lists, tables, and markup.

The history of the apostrophe is entangled with the history of the elision mark
in Greek.[fn:apostrophe]

[fn:apostrophe] The apostrophe entered English in the sixteenth century, initially
to mark the omission of a letter (as in /don't/ for /do not/). Its use as a
possessive marker came later and was inconsistently applied until the nineteenth
century.

Key functions of the modern apostrophe:

- *Contraction*: marking omitted letters (=it's=, =don't=, =they're=).
- *Possession*: indicating ownership (=the dog's lead=, =the dogs' leads=).
- *Plurals* (controversial): some style guides permit =the 1990's= — others forbid it.
- *Omission in poetry*: =o'er=, ='twas=, =ne'er=.

Misuse statistics[fn:7]:

| Error type                  | Frequency (%) |
|-----------------------------+---------------|
| Greengrocer's apostrophe    |            42 |
| Missing possessive          |            28 |
| /It's/ vs /its/ confusion   |            19 |
| Other                       |            11 |

[fn:7] These figures are illustrative and not derived from a real corpus study.
See [cite:@crystal2003] for actual quantitative analysis of punctuation errors
in learner writing.
